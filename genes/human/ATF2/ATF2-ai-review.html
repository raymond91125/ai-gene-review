Gene Description

ATF2 (Activating Transcription Factor 2, also known as CRE-BP1 or CREB2) is a phosphorylation-regulated bZIP transcription factor of the ATF/CREB subfamily. It binds CRE (5'-TGACGTCA-3') and AP-1 (5'-TGACTCA-3') DNA elements as homodimers or heterodimers with JUN family members and other bZIP proteins. Its transcriptional activity is activated by MAPK-mediated phosphorylation at Thr-69/Thr-71 downstream of stress, cytokine, and growth factor signaling (JNK, p38, ERK). ATF2 possesses intrinsic histone acetyltransferase (HAT) activity toward histones H2B and H4, though the physiological significance of this activity has been debated. Beyond transcription, ATF2 participates in the DNA damage response through ATM-dependent phosphorylation at Ser-490/Ser-498, which recruits it to IR-induced foci independently of its DNA-binding domain. ATF2 subcellular localization is regulated by PKC-epsilon; under genotoxic stress, ATF2 can translocate to the mitochondrial outer membrane where it perturbs HK1-VDAC1 complexes to promote apoptosis. ATF2 regulates genes involved in stress response, cell proliferation, DNA repair, and redox homeostasis.

0) Target verification (critical)

The requested target is human activating transcription factor 2 (ATF2), UniProt P15336, a basic leucine zipper (bZIP) transcription factor in the ATF/CREB family. The literature synthesized here consistently discusses ATF2 as a bZIP transcription factor that binds CRE/AP-1–type DNA elements and is regulated by MAPK and DNA-damage kinases, matching the UniProt description and avoiding conflation with other “ATF” family members. (amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 2-3, liu2025activatingtranscriptionfactors pages 2-3)

1) Key concepts and definitions (current understanding)

1.1 Molecular function

ATF2 is a phosphorylation-regulated transcription factor that controls gene expression by binding DNA as homo- or heterodimers via its bZIP domain. Partner choice modulates DNA-binding specificity and transcriptional output. (amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 2-3, liu2025activatingtranscriptionfactors pages 2-3)

1.2 DNA elements and dimerization logic

ATF family bZIP proteins include (i) a basic region for DNA binding and (ii) a leucine zipper for dimerization. Dimerization is a major determinant of which motifs are recognized: ATF homodimers or CREB-family heterodimers preferentially bind CRE consensus motifs, whereas heterodimerization with AP-1 proteins (notably c-JUN) redirects binding toward AP-1 sites. (liu2025activatingtranscriptionfactors pages 2-3)

Consistent with this, ATF2 is described as binding ATF/CRE motifs and functioning as homodimers or heterodimers with AP-1/ATF proteins. (amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 2-3)

1.3 Core regulatory concept: “phosphoswitch” control by MAPKs

A central mechanistic concept for ATF2 is multisite phosphorylation in its transactivation region that tunes transcriptional output downstream of stress/growth-factor signaling (MAPKs). In cancer- and stress-related settings, the most commonly referenced MAPK-regulated activation is at Thr69/Thr71. (amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 2-3)

1.4 Additional concept: transcription factor participation in DNA damage response (DDR)

ATF2 is unusual among transcription factors in that it is also described as participating early in DDR via ATM-dependent phosphorylation at the C-terminus and recruitment to DNA repair foci, suggesting an adaptor/sensor role beyond classical transcriptional regulation. (amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 2-3)

2) Mechanistic functional annotation: regulation, localization, pathways

2.1 Upstream activators and post-translational modifications (PTMs)

A consolidated set of ATF2 PTMs and upstream kinases (from a 2023 review focused on mechanistic oncology contexts) includes: (amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 2-3)

  • MAPK/ERK axis: ERK1/2-mediated phosphorylation reported at Thr71. (amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 2-3)
  • VRK1: phosphorylation at Ser62 and Thr73. (amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 2-3)
  • PKC: phosphorylation at Ser121, Ser340, Ser367. (amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 2-3)
  • ATM (DDR): phosphorylation at Ser490 and Ser498, linked to intra-S checkpoint and DNA repair foci localization. (amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 2-3)

ATF2 is also placed downstream of oncogenic signaling routes (e.g., RalGDS/Ras/ERK/Src) that activate the transcription factor and feed into proliferation and stress programs. (amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 2-3)

2.2 Subcellular localization

Nuclear localization is primary and expected for a transcription factor. In DNA damage contexts, ATM phosphorylation (Ser490/Ser498) is described to direct ATF2 into DNA repair foci, where it colocalizes with Rad50, Nbs1, and Mre11. (amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 2-3)

2.3 Key pathways

ATF2 functions as a node at the intersection of:

  • Stress/inflammation MAPK signaling: p38/JNK/ERK pathways converge on ATF2 to regulate AP-1/CRE transcriptional programs. (amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 7-8, amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 2-3)
  • DNA damage response (ATM-dependent): ATF2 phosphorylation at Ser490/Ser498 supports DDR and genomic stability. (amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 3-4, amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 2-3)
  • Inflammatory epithelial signaling: ATF2 is implicated downstream of inflammatory triggers (e.g., IL-1β → ERK → ATF2 → MMP3 in cancer/inflammation-linked remodeling contexts). (amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 7-8, amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 2-3)

3) Downstream genes, biological processes, and cellular roles

3.1 Canonical transcriptional outputs in cancer and tissue remodeling

In cancer-focused synthesis, ATF2 is linked to transcriptional regulation of multiple genes and programs relevant to proliferation, invasion, EMT, and therapy response, including MMP2/MMP13/MMP3, cyclin A, cyclin D1, aromatase, and COX2 in specific contexts (breast cancer and related models). (amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 4-5, amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 6-7)

3.2 Redox homeostasis and glutathione biology (cell function and therapeutic potency)

A 2023 primary study in human mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs) provides a mechanistic program in which activated ATF2 integrates with CREB1–NRF2 signaling to induce genes involved in glutathione synthesis and redox cycling, including GCLC, GCLM, GSR, and PRDX1, supporting high intracellular glutathione (GSH) and MSC functional properties. (ju2023activatingtranscriptionfactor2 pages 6-10, ju2023activatingtranscriptionfactor2 media 51b94e31)

4) Recent developments and latest research (prioritizing 2023–2024)

4.1 2023: ATF2 controls MSC antioxidant capacity and impacts asthma-relevant therapeutic efficacy

Ju et al., 2023-02 (Experimental & Molecular Medicine; https://doi.org/10.1038/s12276-023-00943-z) report that ATF2 supports GSH dynamics in human MSCs and that ATF2 silencing impairs MSC proliferation, migration-related phenotypes, and anti-inflammatory capacities, with rescue by exogenous GSH supplementation. Quantitative study details include multiple assay sample sizes (e.g., n=5–7 for several in vitro readouts; and in vivo cohort sizes n=20 for BALF cellular counts/lung RQ-PCR and n=10 for BALF cytokines), and in vivo dosing of 3×10^5 MSCs i.v. (ju2023activatingtranscriptionfactor2 pages 6-10)

A pathway schematic in this paper summarizes AA2G or cAMP signaling → ATF2/CREB1 → NRF2 → GSH gene program (GCLC/GCLM/GSR/PRDX1). (ju2023activatingtranscriptionfactor2 media 51b94e31)

4.2 2023: ATF2–c-JUN transcriptional module drives NSCLC invasion/metastasis downstream of JNK

Sun et al., 2023-11 (BMC Cancer; https://doi.org/10.1186/s12885-023-11612-9) provide a mechanistic NSCLC model in which ANKRD49 elevates p-JNK, leading to activation of ATF2 and c-JUN, which interact in the nucleus and bind MMP2/MMP9 promoters (immunoprecipitation and ChIP), thereby promoting migration/invasion. Quantitative descriptors available from the accessible text include n=9 paired fresh NSCLC tissues for qPCR and multiple inhibitor conditions (e.g., 10 µM SP600125 (JNK inhibitor) and 10 µM SB203580 (p38 inhibitor), each 1 h pretreatment) with significance thresholds indicated (P<0.05, P<0.01, **P<0.001) rather than exact p-values. (sun2023ankrd49promotesthe pages 5-9)

4.3 2023: ATF2 as a node in TAK1→MAPK signaling relevant to intestinal barrier integrity and inflammation

Jang et al., 2023-08 (Frontiers in Pharmacology; https://doi.org/10.3389/fphar.2023.1217111) report that sinapic acid directly binds TAK1 and inhibits downstream inflammatory signaling including NF-κB and MAPK/ATF-2 pathways in Caco-2 epithelial models and DSS colitis in mice. The accessible text provides dosing/time-course detail: sinapic acid 12.5–50 µM in vitro; TEER measured across 0.5–24 h after stimulation; and FITC-dextran permeability assessed at 12 and 24 h. (jang2023sinapicacidalleviates pages 2-3)

4.4 2024: ATF2 as a candidate biomarker and pathway-linked gene in ESCC chemoradiotherapy response/prognosis

Cui et al., 2024-11 (Frontiers in Molecular Biosciences; https://doi.org/10.3389/fmolb.2024.1512715) identify ATF2 among “key genes” associated with chemoradiotherapy response and prognosis in esophageal squamous cell carcinoma and connect high ATF2 expression to EMT pathway upregulation. ATF2 is included in a prognostic nomogram together with TNM stage and two other genes. The accessible excerpt does not include detailed effect sizes/p-values but documents the intended clinical application and mechanistic links (e.g., Rac1–p38–ATF2 axis; ATF2 binding GLUT3 promoter in CRC EMT in cited literature). (cui2024identificationofkey pages 9-10)

4.5 2023 (review synthesis): dual oncogene/tumor-suppressor framing in breast cancer

A 2023 narrative review emphasizes that ATF2 can behave as an oncogene in some contexts and as a tumor suppressor in others (notably ER+ breast cancer), with endocrine therapy resistance mechanisms described. It reports that tamoxifen induces dose-dependent phosphorylation of ATF2 and that phospho-ATF2 is linked to prognosis in tamoxifen-treated ER+ patients (review-level statement without extractable HRs in the provided text). (amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 1-1, amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 6-7)

5.1 Biomarker and stratification uses (translational direction)

5.2 Therapeutic pathway targeting concepts

  • MAPK→ATF2 axis modulation is discussed as a strategy for malignancy and therapy resistance contexts (e.g., JNK/p38/ERK control of ATF2 activity; proposed combination strategies in breast cancer). (amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 5-6, amarah2023emergingrolesof pages 3-4)

In MSC therapy-relevant contexts, ATF2 emerges as a determinant of therapeutic potency via redox control; priming strategies (e.g., AA2G) that elevate ATF2 activity are proposed to improve MSC function. (ju2023activatingtranscriptionfactor2 pages 6-10, ju2023activatingtranscriptionfactor2 media 51b94e31)

core_functions:
- description: >-
    Sequence-specific DNA-binding transcription factor that binds CRE
    (5-prime-TGACGTCA-3-prime) and AP-1 (5-prime-TGACTCA-3-prime) elements as
    homo- or heterodimers with JUN family and other bZIP proteins, activating
    transcription by RNA polymerase II. Direct substrate and effector of MAPK
    cascade signaling (JNK, p38, ERK); phosphorylation at Thr-69/Thr-71 by
    these kinases activates transcriptional activity in response to stress,
    cytokines, and growth factors. Also participates in the DNA damage response
    through ATM-dependent phosphorylation at Ser-490/Ser-498, recruiting to
    IR-induced foci and promoting S-phase checkpoint signaling. Primarily
    localized to the nucleus; under genotoxic stress, can translocate to the
    mitochondrial outer membrane where it promotes apoptosis by perturbing
    HK1-VDAC1 complexes.
